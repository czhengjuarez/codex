# Discovery

Discovery is the continuous process of understanding customer problems before committing to solutions. It is not a phase. It does not end when development starts.

---

## Why Discovery Matters

Most product failures are not execution failures — they are discovery failures. We built the wrong thing, or we built it for the wrong people, or we solved a problem customers don't actually have.

Discovery is cheap. Building the wrong thing is expensive.

---

## The Core Loop

```
Observe → Hypothesize → Test → Learn → Repeat
```

Each loop should be as short as possible. A discovery sprint is measured in days, not weeks.

---

## Customer Interviews

### When to use them

- When you don't understand the problem space
- When you're choosing between two directions
- When a feature has underperformed and you don't know why

### How to do them well

1. **Recruit the right people.** Interview the person with the problem, not the person who approved the budget.
2. **Ask about the past, not the future.** "Tell me about the last time you tried to do X" yields more than "Would you use a feature that does Y?"
3. **Listen for workarounds.** The manual process customers use today is the feature you should build.
4. **Don't pitch.** The interview is for learning, not for selling.

### Minimum bar

Talk to at least 3 customers before writing a spec. Talk to at least 5 before committing to a direction.

---

## Prototyping

A prototype is anything that tests an assumption faster than building the real thing.

| Assumption type | Prototype approach |
|----------------|-------------------|
| "Customers have this problem" | Customer interview |
| "Customers would use this solution" | Clickable mockup |
| "This interaction makes sense" | Usability test |
| "This will perform at scale" | Load test / spike |
| "Customers will pay for this" | Pricing conversation |

Use the cheapest prototype that can falsify the assumption.
